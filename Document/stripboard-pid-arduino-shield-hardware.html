A stripped-down PID controller purpose designed for a DIY build, suitable for governing temperatures up to 125°C. Just bring your Arduino!



DIY PID Controller


A PID controller is like a kind of fancy thermostat, but can be used to control pretty much any kind of process, not just heating and cooling. This project was inspired by the osPID (open source PID) and is indebted to that pioneering device in every possible way.

The goal was to design a PID controller that could be built from individual components with minimal skill and know-how. With this in mind, the hardware design attempted to recreate as much as possible of the functionality of osPID on a hand-soldered prototype board using through-hole components. The firmware is correspondingly tweaked to take account of the different temperature sensors and pinouts, and the front end software is borrowed directly from the osPID.

Although intended as a hobby project, it is definitely more than just a toy. With the addition of an SSR module and suitable cookware, the stripboard PID shield could be used directly in applications such as sous vide food preparation, brewing, and making the best espresso this side of Milan.


Hardware Design


This post is the first of a series of three, and describes how to build the PID shield itself. Future posts will give details on the Arduino firmware required to operate the PID controller, and how to install and use the software front end.

The stripboard layout was designed using the freeware version of Eagle, following this useful guide. It would have been nice to spaced things out a little more, but I chose to constrain the board to a standard size so that the the shield would not dwarf the Arduino underneath.

To keep things simple, all the materials (apart from the Arduino itself) can all be sourced from Tayda. I have never been let down by the quality of Tayda components, despite their low prices — and their shipping charges are low, too.

Two possibilities are implemented for the temperature sensor: an analogue thermistor, or the digital DS18B20. These options limit the maximum temperature to 125°C or so. The original osPID uses a MAX31855 to interface with a thermocouple, allowing much higher temperatures, but this is outside the scope of the current project.


Build


  1. Orient the stripboard with one long side to the front and the copper tracks on the top. Cut tracks in the positions shown on the diagram below using a 1/8" drill bit or craft knife. It's a good idea to check with the continuity tester setting on your multimeter.



  2. Now flip the stripboard over the long axis. The short edges on the left and right should stay on the same side, but what was the front edge should now be at the back and the copper tracks should now be on the bottom. Solder in the male and female pin headers and SIP sockets as shown below. Placing these headers first makes it slightly easier to get the other components and wire jumpers in the right place. Soldering is much easier with a temperature controlled soldering station. For safety reasons,  I recommend using lead free solder.



  3. Cut wire jumpers to size and solder in place. Solid core wire is preferable for stripboard because the corners can be bent nicely so it lies flat: flat nose jewelry pliers are useful for this. It is helpful to colour code the wires. I suggest using black wire for anything connecting the GND, red wire for connecting to 5V, and white wire for everything else. Use flush cutters to trim the wires after soldering.



  4. Solder the axial resistors in place. This will be easy after the practice you have had soldering the jumpers!



  5. Solder in the larger components. Be sure to orient the symmetrical components correctly. Make sure the positive and negative terminals of the LEDs and buzzer are in the correct holes, and that the transistor is facing the right way. The terminal blocks should have the wire clamps facing outward.




  6. Now make the temperature probes. Solder red and black stranded wires to the thermistor pins. (These can be twisted if desired to reduce noise on the input). Solder red, black, and yellow stranded wires to the DS18B20 VDD, GND, and DATA pins, respectively. Straddle the SIP sockets with the legs of a 10 KΩ 1% resistor. Insert the chosen temperature probe into the appropriate terminals of the 3-position input terminal block. The thermistor goes between VDD and DATA, forming a voltage divider with the 10 KΩ 1% resistor in the SIP sockets. Alternatively, connect all 3 wires from the DS18B20 to the appropriate terminal, with the 10 KΩ 1% resistor acting as a pull-up on the DATA line. If the temperature probe is going to get immersed in water, it is probably advisable to put it in some kind of thermowell.


  7. Lastly, label the switches and fit the LCD display into the female headers. The PID shield is complete. Just add your Arduino!


Materials


  • 93x53mm stripboard (copper)
  • 1' black 22 AWG solid hook-up wire
  • 1' red 22 AWG solid hook-up wire
  • 1' white 22 AWG solid hook-up wire
  • 10' red 22 AWG stranded hook-up wire
  • 10' black 22 AWG stranded hook-up wire
  • 5' yellow 22 AWG stranded hook-up wire
  • 0.1" pitch male pin header strip
  • 0.1" pitch female pin header strip
  • 0.1" pitch SIP socket strip
  • 10 x 330 Ω resistor 5%
  • 10 x 470 Ω resistor 5%
  • 10 x 1 KΩ resistor 5%
  • 10 x 10 KΩ resistor 1%
  • 4 x tactile switch
  • 5mm red LED
  • 5mm yellow LED
  • 2N3904 transistor
  • PCB mount piezoelectric buzzer
  • 10 KΩ trimpot
  • 16x2 character LCD display with blue backlight
  • NTC 10 KΩ thermistor
  • DS18B20 digital temperature sensor
  • screw terminal block 5mm pitch, 3 positions
  • screw terminal block 5mm pitch, 2 positions


Recommended Tools


  • Irwin self-adjusting wire stripper
  • Hakko flush diagonal cutters
  • Flat nose jewelry pliers
  • Aoyue 936 soldering station
  • lead free rosin core solder, 0.7 mm diameter
